My thoughts are directed toward the polish level of writing, to use Franklin’s (1986) term. It is a stage addressed late in the writing process. One can find an abundance of writing books about research writing and literary writing with rules and principles to follow concerning good sentence construction and word choice. Wolcott (2001), a qualitative ethnographer, for example, talks about honing editorial skills to eliminate unnecessary words, deleting the passive voice, scaling down qualifiers, eliminating overused phrases, and reducing excessive quotations, use of italics, and parenthetical comments. The following additional ideas about active voice, verb tense, and reduced “fat” can strengthen and invigorate scholarly writing for dissertation and thesis proposals. • Use the active voice as much as possible in scholarly writing (APA, 2001). According to the literary writer, Ross-Larson (1982), “If the subject acts, the voice is active. If the subject is acted on, the voice is passive” (p. 29).
